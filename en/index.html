Arrival

The whiteboard photo.
Everyone took it. No one read it the same way.

The offsite ended. Everyone nodded. Then everyone went back to their desks.

scroll slowly

This happens more often than most teams admit.

the problem

One photo. Three different roadmaps.

Two weeks later, two teams discover they built against different versions of the same plan.
A month later, a disagreement resurfaces that everyone thought the offsite had settled.
Three months later, someone asks: when did we actually decide this?

Everyone was right, each in their own way.

This is where Nasj starts.

position

The usual fix doesn't touch the problem.

They call in an agency to sharpen the message. Or a consultant to force a decision from the top. Sometimes it all gets written into a report that sits in an archive. But the roadmaps that came out of that one photo stay exactly as divergent as they were. Nasj works there specifically — on the picture itself, not the message, the decision, or the archive.

The same divergence shows up in a coalition, a campaign, or a research team — not just company meetings.

So the question is: who goes back and rebuilds the picture that broke apart in that room?

the loom

Not a production line. A loom.

We go back to that meeting, and ask each person in it one question: what exactly did you take away?

We write the answers side by side, and see exactly where they contradict.

Then we build one version that everyone actually agrees to — not just nods at.

And from that work, something usable comes out.

woven objects

Woven objects, not products.

A map of exactly where the picture diverged. A two-hour session that rebuilds one version. A one-page brief everyone actually signs — twice, and means it.

field & evidence

We thought one map would be enough. It wasn't.

We presented the first map the following week. Three of five people objected immediately — a disagreement that never came up in the original meeting. We rewrote it twice before everyone actually agreed. This is what we show — not confidence, but the correction that came before it.
